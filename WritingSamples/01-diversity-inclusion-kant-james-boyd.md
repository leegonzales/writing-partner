## Kant's Moral Imperative

Kant's categorical imperative is a moral rule that states that one should always act in a way that they would be willing to make into a universal law. In other words, one should act only in accordance with principles that they would be willing to see become a general rule for everyone to follow. This principle is especially relevant in the context of diversity and inclusion. It is fundamental when considering how to create fair and equitable rules, processes, and culture.

I think we can all agree that treating all individuals with dignity and respect, ensuring that everyone is treated as an end in themselves, rather than a means to an end, is not only a moral obligation but how each of us would prefer to be treated. It is essential for creating a just and equitable business environment. For example, in a business setting, this principle would mean that all employees should be treated with respect, regardless of their race, gender, sexual orientation, or any other characteristic. It would also mean that all employees should be given equal opportunities to succeed and advance within the company. By treating all employees with dignity and respect and by creating a culture of inclusion, businesses can foster a more productive and engaged workforce.

Kant believed, as do I, that individuals have inherent value and dignity. Therefore it is important to consider the impact of our actions on others and to make sure that we are not treating others as means to an end. To live in a just world this principle must be universally applied in how we treat employees, suppliers, customers, the community, and the environment in which the business operates. By considering the impact of our actions on others, we can ensure that we are not exploiting or harming others for our own gain. Again, it is basic, but it is how we all want to be treated, right? It is the world we all would like to live in, right?

## Radical Empiricism

The value of diversity and inclusion in decision-making and problem-solving can be understood through William James' concept of radical empiricism. This concept emphasizes the importance of experience and observation in the acquisition of knowledge (sense-making and meaning-making).

In terms of diversity and inclusion, this means that individuals should be open to learning from the experiences and perspectives of people who are different from them, in order to gain a more comprehensive and accurate understanding of the world. By embracing diverse perspectives, teams can better understand the complexities of any given situation or problem. For example, a team comprised solely of men may have difficulty understanding how gender bias affects certain decisions or outcomes. By including women on the team, they can gain insight into how gender bias may be impacting their decisions or outcomes.

You want to make better decisions, right? Actually, you want to make decisions that are more coherent and fit for your current situation, right? Because you want to survive and thrive, right?

Additionally, by having members with different backgrounds and experiences on a team, they can bring unique perspectives that could lead to innovative solutions to problems or new ways of looking at an issue. Furthermore, by having a diverse team with different backgrounds and experiences, teams are better equipped to make informed decisions that take into account all stakeholders involved. Diverse teams will also be more likely to consider multiple points of view when making decisions as well as identify potential unintended consequences that could arise from their actions. This leads to better decision-making as well as improved problem solving capabilities since teams are able to think outside their constraining frames when it comes to tackling difficult issues.

To do this well, people and teams must commit to a culture of transparency, openness, trust, and humility. Managers and leaders must strive to listen, and commit to creating psychological safety to foster the ability to truly see and understand the world. This is not merely the right thing to do; it is fundamentally necessary for the creation of durable competitive advantage and starkly put survival.

## Situational Awareness and the OODA Loop

John Boyd developed the OODA loop, his conceptual framework for competition, during his time as a fighter pilot when he initially could not figure out how it was that American fighter pilots flying objectively weaker, and slower fighter jets prevailed against their faster and more powerful adversaries. Was it training? Certainly, but more so, it was situational awareness, and the follow on sense making, meaning making, and action that followed. It was the speed or tempo of running OODA which made all the difference.

OODA stands for Observe, Orient, Decide, and Act. This means that in order to effectively navigate any situation or problem, one must first observe their environment, orient themselves with the current situation, decide on an appropriate course of action, and then act on it.

In terms of diversity and inclusion, this means that having a diverse team with different backgrounds and experiences is essential for effectively navigating the OODA loop. A diverse team is better equipped to observe their environment more accurately as they have access to multiple perspectives, leading to better decision-making.

This parallax in seeing the world is key!

Having members with diverse backgrounds and experiences on a team also means that they have a wider range of perspectives to draw upon when assessing a situation and determining the best course of action. This diversity of perspective allows the team to consider multiple viewpoints and options when making decisions, leading to more informed and well-rounded decisions.

Furthermore, by having a diverse team with different backgrounds and experiences, the team is better equipped to anticipate and respond to the consequences of their actions, as well as to identify potential unintended consequences that may arise. This allows them to adjust and take corrective actions as needed, leading to better outcomes.

There are several key traits that enable teams to thrive when running the OODA loop quickly: openness within a team means people are more likely to effectively process feedback from others which can help them act more quickly and effectively in response to changing circumstances. High trust, low ego environments mean people will be able to listen, and hear new ideas that might conflict with their own views, ideas, and more deeply identities.

By embracing diversity, inclusion, humility, and openness within teams, businesses can create an environment of psychological safety where everyone feels heard, valued, and appreciated, ultimately leading to a greater likelihood of survival, success, and thriving.
